Endotoxins

### **What is an endotoxin?**

- Found in the **outer membrane** of Gram-negative bacteria like *E. coli*.
- They’re heat-stable and not destroyed by typical sterilization like autoclaving.
- Even tiny amounts can trigger fever, inflammation, or *endotoxin shock* in animals or humans.

---

### **Why test peptides for endotoxins?**

- During peptide synthesis, handling, or lyophilization, bacterial contamination can occur.
- For **research peptides**, endotoxin testing ensures the material is free from these toxic residues, especially if used in **in vivo** research models.
- Regulatory labs (like 503B pharmacies) have strict limits—often **< 0.25 EU/mL** for injectable solutions.

---

### **Testing methods**

1. **LAL Assay (Limulus Amebocyte Lysate)**
   - Uses enzymes from horseshoe crab blood that clot in the presence of endotoxins.
   - Can be *gel-clot*, *turbidimetric*, or *chromogenic* (color-change) formats.
   - Most common in both research and pharmaceutical testing.
2. **Recombinant Factor C (rFC) Assay**
   - Synthetic alternative to LAL—no animal harvesting.
   - Fluorescent readout; same sensitivity.

---

### **Result format**

- Reported in **Endotoxin Units (EU)** per milliliter or per milligram of sample.
- Example: “< 0.05 EU/mg” means the peptide meets extremely low endotoxin limits.
