What Are COAs (Certificates of Analysis)?

![](/images/content/screenshot-2025-09-27-at-6-27-28-pm-176x300.png)

*Why You Should Never Inject a Peptide Without One*
If you’re using peptides—or thinking about it—there’s one document you need to understand:
**The COA.**
That stands for **Certificate of Analysis**, and it’s the lab report that tells you exactly what’s in your vial.
If a peptide doesn’t have a COA, don’t use it.
Plain and simple.

### **🧪 What Is a COA?**

A **Certificate of Analysis** is a quality control document from a third-party lab that confirms:

- **Purity** of the compound (usually expressed as a percentage)
- **Identity** of the compound (so you know it’s what the label says it is)
- **Absence of contaminants** (like bacteria, heavy metals, solvents, or endotoxins)
- **Batch number** (so you know the report matches your vial)

In short, it’s the peptide’s **receipt + lab report + ID card**—all in one.

### **🔍 What to Look For**

When reviewing a COA, here are the key things to check:
✅ **Batch number** — It should match what’s on your vial or box
✅ **Purity %** — You want 98% or higher
✅ **Test results** — Look for HPLC, MS, or endotoxin testing
✅ **Lab name** — It should be from a legit third-party testing lab, not the seller themselves
✅ **Date tested** — Make sure it’s recent and not expired
If the COA doesn’t check these boxes, it’s a red flag.

### **⚠️ No COA = No Trust**

A peptide without a COA is like a car without a VIN. You have no idea where it came from, what’s in it, or what you’re putting into your body.
Some sketchy vendors will send a generic “example COA” or one that doesn’t match your vial’s batch number. That’s not good enough.
**You need a real, specific, traceable COA tied to your product.**

### **👀 Why This Matters**

You’re injecting this stuff into your body.
You should know for sure:

- It’s clean
- It’s real
- It’s dosed correctly
- And it’s not filled with junk, bacteria, or worse

The COA is your proof. No guesswork. No crossing fingers.

### **Quick Example**

Say you get a vial labeled “Tesamorelin.”
Without a COA, it could be:

- Under-dosed
- A completely different peptide
- Full of contaminants
- Or nothing at all

But with a real COA, you know:
> Batch #TS-0289

> Tested 6/15/25

> 98.7% purity

> Endotoxin-free

> Verified by XYZ Labs

Now you’re not guessing—you’re **confident**.

### **The Bottom Line**

**Never inject a peptide that doesn’t come with a COA.**
No matter how pretty the label looks.
No matter how many good reviews the seller has.
No matter how much you want to believe it’s fine.
A real COA protects your health and proves the product is legit. Anything less is a gamble—and it’s not worth the risk.
