### Summary

This video features an in-depth interview with Peter Magic, founder of Janoshik, a leading testing laboratory in the peptide industry. Peter shares the origins of his company, its growth, and the evolution of peptide testing over the years. Starting from humble beginnings as a self-taught analyzer while addressing his own fitness challenges, Peter built Janoshik into a globally recognized lab that processes about 100 peptide tests daily. The interview delves into the range of tests his lab offers—including mass spectrometry, endotoxin, sterility, and heavy metal testing—highlighting which tests are essential and which are often overrated. Peter dispels common myths around peptide handling, such as the sensitivity of peptides to shaking and the use of bacteriostatic water for reconstitution, emphasizing that many fears stem from misinformation or poor-quality products. He also discusses peptide shelf life, storage best practices, and the challenges labs face in maintaining accuracy and consistency. The conversation touches on the global peptide market, the importance of group or blind testing to ensure quality, and the strict regulatory environment they navigate. Peter concludes by sharing that Janoshik has a roughly 5% failure rate on peptides tested and expresses his passion for the work and commitment to quality and compliance.

### Highlights

- 🧪 Janoshik processes around 100 peptide tests daily, making it a top lab in the peptide testing industry.
- 🧬 Mass spectrometry (LCMS) is the primary and most reliable test used to assess peptide purity and composition.
- ❌ Heavy metal testing on peptides is generally unnecessary and often overrated.
- 💉 Peptides are far more stable than commonly believed; shaking and reconstitution with bacteriostatic or sterile water are mostly safe practices.
- 🧊 Properly stored peptides can last years in refrigerators and decades in freezers without significant degradation.
- 🌍 Over half of Janoshik’s business comes from the US, followed by China, highlighting a global peptide market.
- 🔍 Group or blind testing by independent customers is crucial to verify peptide quality and combat falsified COAs (Certificates of Analysis).

### Key Insights

- 🛠️ **Self-taught expertise can lead to industry leadership:** Peter Magic’s journey from a self-taught tester with no formal chemistry background to founder of a leading peptide testing lab highlights the power of passion, curiosity, and perseverance. His practical experience and continuous learning enabled Janoshik to become a trusted name worldwide.
- 🧪 **Mass spectrometry (LCMS) is the gold standard for peptide testing:**Janoshik primarily uses liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LCMS) to determine peptide purity and detect impurities or unexpected compounds. This technique is highly sensitive and critical for ensuring product quality, especially for complex or large peptides that are difficult to analyze by other means.
- ❌ **Not all tests are equally valuable—heavy metal testing is often unnecessary:** Despite widespread concern among users about heavy metal contamination, Peter emphasizes that actual contamination cases are virtually nonexistent in peptides. He advises against unnecessary heavy metal testing, which adds cost without meaningful benefit, although some customers opt for it for peace of mind.
- 💉 **Peptide handling myths can harm user confidence:** Peter debunks the myth that peptides are extremely sensitive to shaking or that bacteriostatic water must not be used for reconstitution. He explains that these myths originated as justifications by sellers of low-quality or fake products. In reality, peptides are generally robust to shaking and reconstitution methods when proper sterile techniques are used.
- 🧊 **Peptides are remarkably stable if stored properly:** Janoshik’s testing of decade-old human growth hormone samples stored at room temperature showed only minor degradation, still within an effective range. Properly lyophilized peptides stored refrigerated or frozen can last years or decades. This stability eases concerns about peptide shelf life and storage.
- 🌍 **Global peptide market demands rigorous testing and regulation:**Janoshik’s client base is predominantly in the US and China, reflecting the international nature of peptide production and consumption. The lab’s extensive experience with import/export compliance and regulatory paperwork helps them navigate strict controls, especially in Europe, where peptides are highly regulated.
- 🔍 **Group/blind testing is vital for quality assurance:** Because vendors may share COAs or manipulate documents, independent group testing where anonymous samples are submitted to labs is the best way to verify product authenticity and consistency. This community-driven approach helps hold manufacturers and vendors accountable and builds trust in the peptide user community.
- ⚠️ **Sterility testing is critical and failure rates are notable:** While endotoxin failures are rare, sterility tests fail about 3-5% of the time, highlighting contamination risks in peptide vial production or handling. This underscores the importance of sterility testing to avoid infections or adverse reactions, especially for reconstituted peptides.
- ⏳ **Reconstituted peptides have a limited safe usage window:** Peter recommends using reconstituted peptides within 2-4 weeks, especially when stored refrigerated and handled aseptically. Beyond this period, microbial growth and contamination risks increase, which can cause local reactions even if the peptide itself remains active.
- ⚖️ **Testing variability among labs due to technical nuances:** Differences in equipment calibration, sample preparation, and procedural rigor can cause discrepancies in peptide testing results across labs. Janoshik emphasizes the complexity of maintaining lab accuracy, which requires meticulous calibration and quality controls to avoid errors in purity or mass measurements.
- 🧑‍🔬 **Transparency and regulatory compliance are central to Janoshik’s ethos:**Peter does not use peptides himself to avoid conflicts of interest and maintain regulatory trust. The company prides itself on being a legal importer and operates under strict supervision to ensure compliance with scheduled substance laws, reflecting a commitment to professionalism.
- 💡 **Smaller vial sizes enhance safety and dosing accuracy:** Large peptide vials containing excessive quantities pose overdose risks if mishandled. Janoshik advocates for users to purchase vial sizes that match their dosing needs to avoid accidental excessive intake, which can cause uncomfortable side effects like constipation or nausea.
- 💧 **Water quality for reconstitution matters more than brand:** Peter advises using pharmacy-grade sterile water or saline for peptide reconstitution rather than generic bacteriostatic water, which may vary in quality and contain inconsistent benzyl alcohol levels, potentially causing local irritation or endotoxin concerns.

This interview offers a comprehensive view of the peptide testing industry from a leading expert’s perspective. Importantly, he underscores the value of community-driven group testing and transparency to maintain high standards in a largely unregulated global market.
