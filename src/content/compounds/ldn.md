### 🔹 **What is Low Dose Naltrexone (LDN)?**

**Class:** Opioid receptor antagonist, used **off-label** at a small fraction of its FDA-approved dose. Not a peptide — it's a small-molecule oral tablet — but it comes up constantly in immune and chronic-pain stacks, which is why it's covered here alongside peptide-based options.
**Mechanism:**

- At its FDA-approved dose (50 mg), naltrexone works by fully and continuously blocking opioid receptors — that's the mechanism behind its use in opioid and alcohol dependence treatment.
- At **low dose** (roughly 1.5–4.5 mg), the mechanism flips: naltrexone briefly and partially blocks opioid receptors, and the body responds by rebounding with increased production of endorphins and enkephalins — its own natural pain-relief and mood-regulating compounds.
- This transient blockade is also believed to modulate immune signaling, nudging an overactive immune response toward better balance rather than suppressing it outright.
- Because the two doses work through essentially different mechanisms, LDN's low-dose effects are **not** simply a smaller version of what the drug does at 50 mg — it's a different clinical use case entirely, and one that sits outside the drug's approved indication.

**Primary focus:**

- Chronic pain management, including conditions like fibromyalgia and chronic fatigue syndrome.
- Supportive use in autoimmune conditions such as multiple sclerosis, rheumatoid arthritis, and Crohn's disease.
- Mood support, via the endorphin-rebound effect described above.

---

🔹 **Oral Dosage (standard route)**
**Range:**

- Typically dosed at **1.5–4.5 mg** per day — a small fraction of the 50 mg tablet used for addiction treatment.
- Because no commercial product exists at these low strengths, LDN tablets or capsules are compounded specifically for this use.

**Frequency:**

- Once daily, most often taken at bedtime, since some people notice vivid dreams or mild sleep disruption when starting.

**Rationale:** The whole point of "low dose" is staying below the threshold of continuous receptor blockade — dosing too high can push the effect back toward the standard, non-rebound mechanism and away from the low-dose benefit people are pursuing.

---

🔹 **Cycle Length**
**Common cycle length:** LDN is generally used as an ongoing daily therapy rather than in short cycles, often for months at a time as part of chronic condition management.
**Off-cycle:** No mandated break; use is typically continued as long as it's providing benefit and tolerated well, under provider guidance.

---

✅ **Educational Integration Example (oral)**

- **Example protocol:** Starting at 1.5 mg nightly, titrated upward every 1–2 weeks toward a 4.5 mg target dose as tolerated, for ongoing chronic pain or autoimmune symptom support.
- Titration is common because sensitivity to LDN varies significantly between individuals — starting low and adjusting slowly helps identify each person's optimal dose.

---

🔹 **Stacking Strategy (Educational / Complementary)**
Since LDN's role is largely immune modulation and endorphin support rather than tissue repair or hormone signaling, it's often layered alongside compounds addressing those other angles:
🔸 **Immune & Inflammation Support:**

- **Thymosin Alpha-1, KPV:** Complementary immune-modulating and anti-inflammatory mechanisms that pair conceptually with LDN's immune-balancing effect.

🔸 **Recovery & Tissue Support:**

- **BPC-157:** Often discussed alongside LDN in chronic pain and inflammatory-condition protocols, though the two work through distinct mechanisms.

**Rationale:** LDN doesn't compete with peptide-based immune or recovery compounds — it works through a distinct opioid-receptor mechanism, which is part of why it's frequently considered as a complementary layer rather than a replacement for other immune-support strategies.

<div class="quick-facts">
<p><strong>Studied in Humans?</strong> Yes, though the low-dose, off-label use covered here is supported mostly by smaller trials and observational/practice-based evidence rather than the large-scale trials behind naltrexone's approved indication.</p>
<p><strong>FDA Approved?</strong> Naltrexone itself is FDA-approved — but only at the 50 mg dose for opioid and alcohol dependence. The low-dose use described in this entry is <strong>off-label</strong>; there is no FDA-approved low-dose naltrexone product.</p>
<p><strong>Allowed for 503a Compounding Pharmacy?</strong> Yes — LDN is one of the most commonly compounded off-label preparations, since no commercial product exists at the low strengths this use requires.</p>
