### 🔹 **What is Cagrilintide?**

**Class:** Synthetic peptide—long-acting amylin analogue, designed to mimic the hormone amylin, which is co-secreted with insulin in the pancreas.
**Mechanism:** Binds amylin receptors to slow gastric emptying, suppress appetite, and modulate postprandial glucagon release. Works synergistically with GLP-1 pathways to improve satiety and aid in body weight regulation.
**Primary focus:** Appetite regulation, weight loss, improved glycemic control, fat mass reduction, and metabolic support.
🔹 **Oral Dosage (least common)**
**Range studied/used in research:** Oral bioavailability is extremely low; not used orally.
**Typical protocol:** No oral standard; all human and animal research uses injectable subcutaneous formulations.
**Notes:** Subcutaneous injection is the preferred and effective delivery route. Oral or other non-injectable forms are not practical.

---

🔹 **Injectable / Subcutaneous Dosage (most common)**
**Range:** Research and clinical trials typically use 0.3–4.5 mg per week subcutaneously, depending on titration schedules and combination therapies (e.g., with semaglutide).
**Frequency:** Once weekly; long-acting formulation allows sustained receptor activation and reduced injection frequency.
**Rationale:** Slow-release properties improve tolerability and steady appetite suppression, avoiding peaks that could cause nausea or other side effects.

---

🔹 **Cycle Length**
**Common cycle length:** Typically studied in 12–24 week clinical trials for weight management.
**Off-cycle:** Not formally established; discontinuation is based on treatment goals or clinical evaluation.
**Note:** Cycle duration and dosing adjustments are often guided by weight-loss targets, tolerability, and combination with GLP-1 analogues.

---

🔹 **Optimal Dosing Window**
**Injectable / Subcutaneous:** Once weekly, timing flexible. Many users choose morning or midday to align with active hours and maximize appetite control. Avoid late evening if prone to nausea or digestive sensitivity.
