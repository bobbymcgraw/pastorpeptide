### 🔹 **What is MOTS-C ?**

**Class:** Mitochondrial-derived peptide (16-amino acid peptide encoded by mitochondrial DNA).
**Mechanism:**

- Acts as a **mitochondrial signaling peptide** that regulates metabolism and stress responses.
- Enhances **AMPK activation** → improves glucose uptake, fatty acid oxidation, and insulin sensitivity.
- Supports **mitochondrial biogenesis** and protects against metabolic dysfunction.
- Influences **exercise capacity, endurance, and recovery** by optimizing cellular energy utilization.

**Primary focus:**

- Metabolic optimization (insulin sensitivity, glucose regulation, fat metabolism).
- Exercise performance and endurance.
- Anti-aging and mitochondrial resilience.
- Neuroprotective and cytoprotective roles.

---

🔹 **Oral Dosage:** Not effective; rapidly degraded in the digestive system.

---

🔹 **Injectable / Subcutaneous Dosage (most common)**
**Range:**

- **Typical research use:** 5–15 mg per week, divided into 2–3 doses.
- Common dosing patterns: **5 mg 2–3x per week**, or **10 mg once weekly**.

**Frequency:** Subcutaneous injection, often pre- or post-training for performance focus.
**Rationale:** Regular pulses support mitochondrial adaptation and metabolic benefits without desensitization.

---

🔹 **Cycle Length**

- **Common cycles:** 4–12 weeks.
- Often used in **blocks around training seasons or metabolic reset periods**.
- Breaks of 2–4 weeks are typical before restarting.

---

🔹 **Optimal Dosing Window**

- **Morning or pre-training** is preferred for energy and endurance benefits.
- Can also be taken **post-exercise** to aid recovery and mitochondrial biogenesis.

---

✅ **Educational Integration Example**

- Example protocol: **5 mg MOTS-c, 2x/week (Mon/Thu), subcutaneous injection**, for 8 weeks.
- Paired with: Exercise regimen, balanced nutrition, NAD⁺ support.

⚠️ **Safety Notes:**

- Generally considered well-tolerated in research use.
- Possible mild side effects: injection site irritation, fatigue, or mild headaches.
- Human clinical research is still early—long-term safety not fully established.
